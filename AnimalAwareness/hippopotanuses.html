HIPPOPOTAMUS

The incredible hippo has been in the African ecology for millions of years, originally ranging from the Nile river basin to the Cape. Due to extensive habitat loss, retaliatory killings, and illegal and unreported hunting, the remaining 125,000-18,000 common hippos are currently only found in protected regions. It is known that populations are declining in half of the 29 countries that still have common hippos.

Africa is home to two different hippo species: the large common hippo, which can be found in the wetlands and plains of East Africa, and the diminutive, lone pygmy hippo, which dwells in the woodlands of West Africa. Only 2,000–3,000 pygmy hippos are thought to remain in Africa.

These aquatic mammals need constant access to water to maintain the moisture in their unique skins. Common hippos typically dwell in groups of tens to hundreds, therefore the eradication of a large grazer from the area would have a significant ecological impact. While common hippos are designated as vulnerable and pygmy hippos as endangered, both of their conservation statuses are based on a 2004 estimate suggestion, which is why very little is known about the shy pygmy hippo. It is expected that their numbers will continue to drop based on patterns. Hippos are a magnificent, ancient animal that need to be preserved.
