WALRUSES

The long-lived, sociable walrus lives in the sub-Arctic regions of the Northern Hemisphere. Their most distinctive characteristic are their long tusks, which are an enlarged canine tooth utilized for digging, mating, getting in and out of ice, and fighting. While walruses were an essential part of the original Arctic peoples' way of life, American and European immigrants cruelly exploited them in the 18th and 19th centuries by carelessly collecting their meat and tusks for commercial purposes. Since walruses are listed as Vulnerable, we must take immediate action to stop additional harm to this iconic species.

Since they were historically slaughtered in large numbers, walruses currently struggle to survive due to climate change and the continuous legality of the walrus ivory trade, which has a huge negative influence on their habitats. This well-known Arctic species is threatened and has a bleak outlook. We could offer the walrus a chance to live and prosper if we take immediate action.

The fate of walruses is unknown, and conservationists from all across the Northern Hemisphere concur that they are a species of "special concern" and at risk of being imperiled if precautions aren't done to safeguard them. Walrus populations have been extensively damaged by historical human harvests; they were badly diminished before action was taken to end commercial hunting and trade. However, as we continue to advocate for the conservation of other species that have been exterminated for their ivory, we must keep in mind that by excluding the walrus, we leave them open to being used as a substitute for ivory.
