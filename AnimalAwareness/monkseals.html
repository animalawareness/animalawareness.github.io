MONK SEALS

In their small native range in the Hawaiian Archipelago, Hawaiian monk seals are severely threatened by declining habitats, marine pollution, disease, and human-seal conflict. Other warm-water monk seal species are extinct, and presently there are just 1,300–1,400 Hawaiian monk seals left.

The numbers of Hawaiian monk seals are generally declining as a result of a variety of threats to their existence, such as the state of the ocean, the effects of fishing, and habitat degradation. Within two generations, there has been a more than 20% reduction in population.

To help this limited population recover, it is essential to preserve them from poaching and to safeguard their habitats through strict regulations.

Fortunately, one of the world's most proactive marine mammal recovery efforts is centered on Hawaiian monk seals, according to the IUCN. To keep this historic species protected, we must maintain our current protective trajectory. We must continue to support initiatives to sustain monk seal numbers, reduce human-wildlife conflicts, and support laws that will allow monk seals to thrive because they have such a tiny habitat and face intense competition for resources.
