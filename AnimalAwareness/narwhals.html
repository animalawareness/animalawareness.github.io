NARWHALS

Narwhals—are they real? is one of the first questions that comes up when searching for "narwhals," and the answer is yes, narwhals are very real, but one of the ocean's most unusual creatures are also facing very real threats to their existence. Narwhals are a member of the beluga whale family and are best known for their single long tusk protruding from their nose. These mysterious Arctic sea mammals are capable of diving to depths of up to 2,600 feet and remaining submerged for up to 25 minutes.

The IUCN classifies narwhal populations, which are currently approximately 75,000 and hence Near Threatened, as being in danger of further decline in the absence of adequate protection. Narwhals are still intensively targeted in Canada and Greenland, and their populations are threatened by human activity, climate change, and hunting. The implications of climate change on narwhal habitats are still unknown, although scientists are concerned about the possibility. Changes in the ice pack could have a big impact on the health of the narwhal population because of its limited geographic range and highly specialized feeding and habitat preferences. It is unknown, according to the IUCN, whether the prohibition on the trade in narwhal ivory between Greenland and Denmark is really enforced.
