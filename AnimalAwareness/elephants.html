ELEPHANTS

One of the most well-known and intellectual creatures in the world is the elephant. They form lifelong bonds with their family members and even grieve for the deceased. The elephant population, which were formerly widespread in Asia and Africa, have suffered greatly over the past century as a result of poachers killing one elephant every 15 minutes to feed the massive and lucrative illegal ivory trade.

Elephants of both kinds are suffering. African elephant populations were estimated to be between 3-5 million strong in the 1930s and 1940s. But after widespread slaughter, their numbers have been sharply declining across the continent. 30,000 African elephants were unlawfully killed for their tusks in 2012 alone. Only about 470,000 African elephants are thought to still exist in the wild today.

Even worse is the condition of Asian elephants. There are only thought to be 20,000–25,000 Asian elephants left in the wild today, making them an endangered species.

Poaching and habitat degradation are the two biggest challenges to these significant creatures. We will continue to lose these precious animals as long as the illicit ivory trade is permitted to exist. What does it say about our capacity to save the rest of our biodiversity if we are unable to safeguard the biggest terrestrial animal on the planet? Elephants' future is in our hands, and the continual desire for things made of ivory puts them in constant risk.
