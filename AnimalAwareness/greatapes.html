GREAT APES

Genetically, behaviorally, and physically, great apes resemble humans more than any other animal. They establish close relationships with their families, cooperate when using tools, and interpret body language and gestures similarly. They are an essential species to the ecology of the tropical forests where they have traditionally lived. Despite the fact that Great Ape species are protected by national and international law, these primates are nonetheless poached for their meat and other body parts. 60 percent of gorillas have disappeared in the last few decades due to sickness and poaching. Gorillas, chimpanzees, orangutans, and bonobos are all Great Apes that are either Endangered or Critically Endangered and are experiencing a rapid decrease.

In the rife illicit wildlife trade that extends from Africa and Asia to the coastlines of the United States, great apes are killed for their bushmeat and exchanged for their parts. Two guys were recently detained in Oregon while attempting to enter the state with an orangutan skull and other animal parts. Due to a surge in commercial logging in gorilla habitats, which is damaging their houses, gorillas are critically endangered, and hunters are utilizing the same routes to kill them to extinction. In order to harvest their meat and use their components in traditional medicine, chimpanzees are killed in the wild. More horrifyingly, the illicit pet trade still exists, particularly for chimpanzees who are kidnapped as newborns and frequently have their moms and other family members killed. In order to lead the way and prevent Great Apes from going extinct entirely, it is crucial that the United States establishes tough wildlife trafficking regulations.
